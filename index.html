Grammys 2026: Bad Bunny Makes History

Now to some glitz and glamour from this year’s Grammy Awards. Bad Bunny made history by scooping the Album of the Year prize. It is the first Spanish-language album ever to win the top award on music’s biggest night.

Other big winners included Kendrick Lamar, Lady Gaga and Billie Eilish. Britain’s Olivia Dean was crowned Best New Artist.

Several artists also used the event to voice their opposition to President Trump’s crackdown on immigration.

First, David Willis reports from Los Angeles.

The rock version of Rosé and Bruno Mars’ hit song “APT” got music’s biggest night off to a dynamic start.

Kendrick Lamar collected the 26th Grammy of his career for his album “GNX”, marking the beginning of a history-making night.

Barely had Olivia Dean finished performing her first UK number-one hit “Man I Need” when she was called back on stage. The 26-year-old used an emotional acceptance speech to address the thorny issue of immigration.

“I am a product of bravery,” she said. “And I think those people deserve to be celebrated.”

Puerto Rican artist Bad Bunny went even further, condemning the actions of US immigration agents before going on to win the top award of the night.

“We are not savages. We are not animals. We are not aliens. We are humans, and we are Americans,” he said.

Another first-time British winner, Lola Young, could hardly contain her excitement. Her acceptance speech mirrored the title of her hit song.

“I don’t have a speech prepared,” she laughed. “I know — it’s messy.”

Cher announced the Record of the Year winner, Kendrick Lamar, but briefly confused the name of the song with the artist.

However, the night ultimately belonged to Bad Bunny — the first Latin artist to win the Grammy for Album of the Year. He dedicated the award to those who had left their homeland to follow their dreams.

Analysis & Themes

Speaking to the BBC, music journalist and broadcaster Mary Mandelfield said the night was deeply political. Many artists, she explained, felt a responsibility to use their platform during a divisive time.

“There is no longer a clear separation between music and politics,” she said. “Artists are under pressure — both from fans and from themselves — to speak out.”

British artists also had a strong showing, with wins across pop, electronic and rock categories, highlighting the global impact of UK music.

Beyond the awards, the night was also a celebration of fashion and self-expression, with artists using style as another way to communicate their identity and beliefs.
